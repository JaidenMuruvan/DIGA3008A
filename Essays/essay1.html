Essay 1: UX & UI Analysis

UX & UI Analysis of Makro

Makro, a large wholesale and retail company that operates both physical stores and an e-commerce platform, serving customers primarily in South Africa and parts of southern Africa. This website is designed to support both individual consumers and business buyers, offering a wide range of products. The products range from electronics and appliances to groceries, office supplies and even liquor. In this essay I will be analysing the websites UI and UX and keep in mind that ethical website design is not just about doing the right thing, instead it’s about sustaining a successful and positive user relationship (Ghanchi, 2021).

The websites navigation bar is consistent at the top of every page which features a top horizontal menu with dropdown options for main categories. It is clear and categorized into product types with their wide range of products and these filtering options helps to refine users searches which is very helpful for users to quickly and easily find what they need. This can also become very overwhelming to the user due to the volume of options displayed simultaneously and the amount of categories and subcategories which makes it difficult to make quick decisions. On a mobile device some menus and drops can become difficult to interact with which then affects the ease of use and the responsiveness of the website. A clear and well-structured primary navigation system is essential to help users locate their goals efficiently without cognitive overload (Torresburriel Estudio, 2024). The navigation is generally intuitive and allows for users to freely browse and search through a large range of categories which all in all supports the user autonomy.

However, the presence of frequent promotional popups and visually dominant banners may push users toward certain products, which can count as a manipulative design which is also a concern outlined by Ghanchi as a violation of user trust (Ghanchi, 2021). The hamburger menu functions adequately on mobile but there is room for improvement on usability by introducing more interactive feedback, such as highlighting icons or different colour text to indicate the user location within the site. Improving these navigation patterns would help accommodate different user behaviours, whether users are just browsing through the website or searching it with specific intent, this will lead to a more overall intuitive and supportive user experience (Torresburriel Estudio, 2024).

The font sizes and contrasts are mostly sufficient for readability with an Arial font used for both headings and body text on the Makro website. This also includes a hierarchy that clearly differentiates between titles, subtitles and paragraph text making the websites information easily digestible. The icons and images usually include labels and context, for example for product with what it is used for. The weaknesses however is the site lacks visible accessibility tools like text resizing or screen reader optimization which limits inclusivity. The poor screen reader compatibility and inadequate keyboard navigation suggests that the needs of the users with disabilities were not fully integrated during the development of this website (Constanza-Chock, 2020). The website would also benefit from improving its secondary and footer navigation to include better access to customer service, delivery policies and frequently asked questions which all feel hidden in the interface.

The responsiveness of the website adapts fairly well across different device such as a desktop, tablet and mobile device. The layout also adjusts to the screen size and maintains the consistent brand look. The mobile site can sometimes be sluggish or unresponsive when loading large images or promotional banners. Some interactive elements such the filter checkboxes or menus can be difficult to tap on smaller screens affecting the usability of the site on mobile devices. The website loading in time is exceptionally well with a loading time of just under 3 seconds and this is consistent even with a mobile device.

Makro’s website reveals several design elements that align with the concept of “dark patterns,” as defined by Mathur et al. While the user flow is easy to navigate in terms of users being able to easily register, sign in and add items to their cart, the site frequently uses urgency cues, such as time-limited deals and stock countdown. This may not always reflect real-time data and while these tactics are common in e-commerce it can manipulate users into making a rushed purchasing decision based on the perceived scarcity, a dark pattern category identified as “urgency” (Mathur et al., 2019). The checkout process does include multiple delivery options like “click and collect” or home delivery but if an item is out of stock it sometimes only appears late in the purchase process. This compromises transparency and could lead to user frustration (Ghanchi, 2021). The website mostly maintains its clarity within their prices and visuals, but ethical issues arise when it comes to stock availability not being disclosed until late in the purchasing process. This process also has many steps which can feel long or confusing especially for a first-time user of the site. This also raises ethical concerns as It involves multiple steps that may subtly nudge users into purchasing marketing subscriptions without their fully confirmed consent. Ghanchi emphasizes that an ethical UX of a website should avoid such manipulations and ensure that their users are always fully aware of what they are opting into and purchasing.

The visual design is consistent with the use of Makro’s brand colours which helps with the brands recognition. However there does lie a key issue in this visual design as the colours contrasting between the text and backgrounds in some promotional sections is insufficient making it difficult for users with visual impairments to read clearly. Reliance on the colour alone to display information, such as stock availability alerts or discount tags, fails to support users with colour vision deficiencies (Vaidya, 2020). The products are clear with images, prices and promotions are well displayed as it is the first thing you see on the home page. With that the homepage feels very content heavy with many promotions and while having promotional content is an expected feature of e-commerce platforms, its excessive presence feels like it is competing for attention. This gives the impression that the brands website prioritizes the sales over the user needs which can reduce focus and impact visual hierarchy. A design justice approach would prioritize co-design with actual users, including small business owners, rural customers, and people with limited digital literacy to ensure that the site meets the broader spectrum of user needs. The lack of these community led engagement contributes to the user experience being technical functional yet failing to empower or fully include all of its target audience. This outlines the importance of moving beyond usability metrics and rather to design processes that are inclusive and rooted in shared decision making (Constanza-Chock, 2020). The site relies heavily on banner ads and sliders which may not always be useful and can also slow down the experience. The use of this can clutter the interface and obstruct the users pathways, making it more difficult for them to focus on their actual goals. These design choices may be effective in driving sales, it also reflects a broader trend in digital commerce where user interfaces are shaped to benefit business outcomes at the potential cost of user trust and informed consent (Mathur et al., 2019).

Overall, in analysing the UI and UX of the Makro website through the lens of ethical design, accessibility, navigation structure and user-centred principles it becomes clear that while the site meets functional standards, there is definitely areas for improvement. It would significantly benefit the website if these ethical gaps were addressed to enhance navigation, responsiveness, trust within the user, accessibility and user prioritization. Makro’s website has a strong foundation and must commit to a more ethical, inclusive and human-centred design approach to ensure that all users, regardless of ability, background or intent, can navigate and interact with its platform easily and effectively.
