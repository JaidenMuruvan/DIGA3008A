Reflection on the readings I selected - relating it to my experience with the internet today

Stephan Harper’s 2010 article “As We May Think” at 65 reflects the ongoing relevance of Vannevar Bush’s seminal 1945 essay As We May Think. Harper basically revisits the ideas from Bush’s original work 65 years later and indicates how his predictions have taken place in computing, hypertext and the internet of today. Bush’s “Memex” (which he described as a theoretical device for storing and organizing information) is much like todays hyperlinks and interconnected web pages. We clearly see this in the way we use search engine and databases to navigate huge amounts of knowledge. We also see in Harper’s article he outlines a key challenge of there being too much information and today’s internet provides an overwhelming flood of data making it more difficult to filter and use reliable sources.

The article “Flash And Its History On The Web by Jay Hoffman which was published on the 7th of August 2017 explores the evolution of Adobe Flash. It was a significant technology in web development and this article takes you through the programs journey. The article outlines the programs transformation from a graphics editor to something that redefined interactivity and design of the internet. The history of Flash is a very good example of modern internet experiences where web technology evolves, and eventually certain programs get replaced by more effective and efficient solutions. The reason for it being replaced was because of the end of browser plugins where users were required to install a browser plugin which became a security risk, and we see with modern browsers it has moved away from plugins as a whole.

Interesting things I have found about the history of the web

A very interesting thing that I have come to realised is that throughout the years of technology evolving and having many new features, it is so important that they continue be updated to fit user needs, otherwise as we have seen in today’s time it ends up being replaced by more suitable solution. The web has come a long way from a simple collection of hyperlinked documents to now a massive AI – driven, multimedia experience.

My goals for this course.

I would like to better my understanding of designing user friendly interfaces and interactions that make navigation through them easy and enjoyable, and I would like to learn how to analyse and interpret user data to measure its effectiveness and adjust or make improvements to it.

Week 2 – Blog Post

Reflection on the Reading

Stuart Moulthrop’s text “You Say You Want a Revolution? Hypertext and the Laws of Media” reflects on early debates surrounding hypertext and its potential to change reading, writing and media consumption and he also outlines how hypertext was first seen as a disruptive force that challenged traditional linear narratives and hierarchical structures in literature, academia and communication. Moulthrop also connects hypertext to Marshall McLuhan’s “Laws of Media” identifying how new media interacts with and transforms older forms of communication and he does this by also applying McLuhan’s four laws of media and hypertext which are, enhancement, obsolescence, retrieval and reversal. It is evident that Moulthrop is suggesting that hypertext is part of this ongoing cultural evolution which is constantly shaped and impacted by economic, social and political forces.

How does it speak to the internet today?

This text is still very relevant to the internet of today, considering the issues such as algorithmic control, digital fragmentation and the commercialization of online spaces. We see this in the internet having information easily accessible to us but corporations such as Google or Meta control the majority of what we see through algorithms and targeted advertising, so instead of free-flowing exchange that early hypertext theorists believed in, digital spaces are shaped on economic interests. Moulthrop outlined hypertexts ability that challenged traditional linear narratives which would allow users to explore texts interactively and while the internet has embraced this through hyperlinks, infinite scrolling and algorithmic feeds this has led to fragmented attention and difficulty in deep reading for the user. So instead of reading a single structured argument, users jump between many media such as links, tweets and short form videos making it harder to come to a unified understanding. Moulthrop used the laws of media to predict that hypertext could become way too complex and that is evident in how the modern web is as there is so much of information that leads to people not trusting many sources and users finding difficulty in finding credible sources. Hypertext was imagined to be an open system where users would freely find and create links and instead of this AI – driven content feeds now dictate what people consume.

Interaction is the essence of how we engage with the world in digital spaces, such as consuming content and engaging with other users. It is the way we shape and are shaped by the vast networks of information, people and algorithms on the web. Structure and URL Scheme - How it relates to Semantic Markup

The exercise emphasizes how we should understand the target audience in order to develop an intuitive, informative and engaging experience. This aligns with semantic markup as it is defined as developing website and creating web content that is meaningful rather than just defining its presentation or look. By knowing how to structure the content around the needs and expectations of users, I can ensure that the website has good functionality and not only visually pleasing. This should also apply in my URL scheme with clear and detailed paths that correspond to each section or each page. Ensuring the websites organization is logical and easy to navigate improves the usability.
