Reflection on the Reading

Stuart Moulthrop’s text “You Say You Want a Revolution? Hypertext and the Laws of Media” reflects on early debates surrounding hypertext and its potential to change reading, writing and media consumption and he also outlines how hypertext was first seen as a disruptive force that challenged traditional linear narratives and hierarchical structures in literature, academia and communication. Moulthrop also connects hypertext to Marshall McLuhan’s “Laws of Media” identifying how new media interacts with and transforms older forms of communication and he does this by also applying McLuhan’s four laws of media and hypertext which are, enhancement, obsolescence, retrieval and reversal. It is evident that Moulthrop is suggesting that hypertext is part of this ongoing cultural evolution which is constantly shaped and impacted by economic, social and political forces.

How does it speak to the internet today?

This text is still very relevant to the internet of today, considering the issues such as algorithmic control, digital fragmentation and the commercialization of online spaces. We see this in the internet having information easily accessible to us but corporations such as Google or Meta control the majority of what we see through algorithms and targeted advertising, so instead of free-flowing exchange that early hypertext theorists believed in, digital spaces are shaped on economic interests. Moulthrop outlined hypertexts ability that challenged traditional linear narratives which would allow users to explore texts interactively and while the internet has embraced this through hyperlinks, infinite scrolling and algorithmic feeds this has led to fragmented attention and difficulty in deep reading for the user. So instead of reading a single structured argument, users jump between many media such as links, tweets and short form videos making it harder to come to a unified understanding. Moulthrop used the laws of media to predict that hypertext could become way too complex and that is evident in how the modern web is as there is so much of information that leads to people not trusting many sources and users finding difficulty in finding credible sources. Hypertext was imagined to be an open system where users would freely find and create links and instead of this AI – driven content feeds now dictate what people consume.

Reflect on interaction and the www: what interaction means to me

Interaction is the essence of how we engage with the world in digital spaces, such as consuming content and engaging with other users. It is the way we shape and are shaped by the vast networks of information, people and algorithms on the web. The www (World Wide Web) transformed interaction from a passive experience to an active experience where you can directly engage with content through comments, hyperlinks and collaborative platforms. The way we interact on the web is a mirror of how we as individuals engage with others, knowledge and society.
