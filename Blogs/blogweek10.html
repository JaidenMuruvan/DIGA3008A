“Internet Access is a Fundamental Right” by S. Kasapis (2020)

In the article “Internet Access is a Fundamental Right,” Kasapis presents an ethically charged argument that puts internet access not merely as a luxury or convenience but more a fundamental human right essential for participation in modern life. Through a mix of philosophical reasoning, references to legal precedent and social justice discourse, this article urges both the governments and global institutions to recognize internet access as vital for democratic participation, education and equality (Kasapis, 2020). From the start of the article, Kasapis anchors the discussion in moral and legal frameworks, drawing parallels between internet access and other socio-economic rights such as education, healthcare and housing. In doing so the author aligns with the broader tradition of rights-based discourse, emphasizing that the digital divide amplifies existing inequalities. It also takes a normative approach in suggesting that digital exclusion results in a social exclusion, reinforcing cycles of poverty and marginalization (Kasapis, 2020).

A key strength of the article lies in its global perspective. Kasapis uses examples ranging from UN declarations to national court rulings such as the 2016 ruling by the UN Human Rights Counsil that condemned intentional internet disruptions, which reinforces the argument that digital access is increasingly being recognised as essential to exercising freedoms of expression, assembly and information. This interlinking of rights underpins the authors thesis that internet access is a gateway right, one that enables the realization of many others. Another element is the articles attention to contemporary relevance. Since it was published in the year 2020, it echoes the heightened urgency brought by the COVID-19 pandemic, during which online access became a lifeline for education, work, healthcare and even civic engagement (Kasapis, 2020). Kasapis uses this context not only to illustrate the practical importance of connectivity but to also challenge outdated conceptions of infrastructure. The author argues that the internet should be treated like electricity or water, which is a public utility essential for modern citizenship (Kasapis, 2020).

However, Kasapis does not stop at the diagnosis, but this article also includes policy recommendations urging government to invest in broadband infrastructure, specifically in rural and underserved or less fortunate communities, treating internet access as part of a universal service obligation. There is also a subtle critique of market driven models, which the author suggests have failed to equitably distribute digital resources, reinforcing structural inequalities. In terms of tone and rhetoric, this article is persuasive yet measured relying more on logical reasoning and legal precedent than emotional appeal. The clarity of structure, problem, justification, evidence, solution all enhances it persuasiveness. Kasapis writes this article not just as an advocate, but as a scholar engaging seriously with ethical and legal dimensions of digital life.

This article is a compelling contribution to ongoing debates about digital rights and justice. By positioning internet access as a fundamental in fully participating in society, Kasapis challenges policymakers to rethink infrastructure, equality and citizenship in the digital age. The piece serves as both a call to action and a moral blueprint for how societies might bridge the digital divide in more just and sustainable ways (Kasapis, 2020).
