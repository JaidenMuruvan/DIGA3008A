Blog Post 11: Week 14

How our code matters as developers in the context of decolonialism, and identifying things in our own coding practice that we must be aware of.

To approach justice and ethics in artistic programming practice I believe that its essential to critically engage with the power structures, assumptions and impacts of the tools and systems we build. Looking at centering community-led design and amplifying marginalized voices is crucial to developing inclusive and sustainable solutions. This approach involves meaningfully engaging those most impacted by social and structural inequities throughout the entire design process, from problem identification to implementation and evaluation. By prioritizing the lived experience, cultural knowledge and priorities of marginalized groups, designers can co-create outcomes that reflect the real needs and aspirations of the communities they serve. Such a participatory process fosters a trustworthy community and encourages long terms ownership of solutions. Design justice challenges traditional power dynamics in design by redistributing decision-making power and valuing voices historically excluded from dominant narratives (Constanza-Chock, 2020). This perspective guides me to prioritize inclusive designs where those who are most affected by technology have a say in its development. For example, in game design or interactive digital art, co-design methods could be implemented with users from underrepresented groups and could test for accessibility such as screen reader compatibility or colourblind-friendly palettes.

Another approach we can look at is deconstructing digital hegemonies, which involves critically examining how power, privilege and inequality are embedded within digital technologies, platforms and infrastructures. Dominant tech cultures often reflect the values and assumptions of a narrow demographic which typically consist of white, male, Western and economically privileged marginalizing or excluding diverse ways of knowing. This hegemony shapes everything from algorithmic bias to platform governance, reinforcing existing social hierarchies and limiting access to equitable participation. By uncovering and challenging these structures, we open up possibilities for alternative and more digital futures. Noble highlights that algorithmic systems are not neutral but often perpetuate systemic racism and other forms of oppression under the guise of objectivity, making critical engagement with digital power structures essential (Noble, 2018). This reminds us that algorithms can be looked at as not neutral and often replicate geopolitical biases and commercial interests. As a developer I think especially looking at it from a justice and ethical standpoint, making searchability, data representation and information architecture more equitable and transparently disclosing algorithmic decision making where applicable will all ensure a better programming practice.

Ensuring ethical practice involves recognizing that digital justice is not just about what we create but also about who can use and benefit from it. Bridging the digital divide requires more than just simply providing access to devices or internet connectivity, it also involves addressing the deeper socio-economic, educational and infrastructural inequalities that limit meaningful digital participation. Marginalized communities often face barriers not only to access but also to digital literacy, cultural relevance and content creation. Efforts to bridge this divide must be community-informed and context specific, ensuring that technology serves as a tool for empowerment rather than a reinforcer of existing inequities. Digital inclusion must be understood as a social and political challenge, not just a technical one, demanding policies and practices that center equity, justice and sustainability in digital access and use (Selwyn, 2010). In our work as developers this means supporting open source and free access and avoiding platform lock in that marginalizes users with limited hardware or connectivity.

References

  • Costanza-Chock, S., 2020. “Design Justice: Community-Led Practices to Build the Worlds We Need.” Cambridge, MA: MIT Press.
  • Noble, S.U., 2018. “Algorithms of Oppression: How Search Engines Reinforce Racism.” New York: NYU Press.
  • Selwyn, N., 2010. “Schools and Schooling in the Digital Age: A Critical Analysis.” London: Routledge.
