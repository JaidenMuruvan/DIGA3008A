"Digital Inequalities in the Age of Artificial Intelligence and Big Data" by Christoph Lutz (2019)

This article by Christoph Lutz provides a timely and nuanced examination of how artificial intelligence or AI, and big data technologies intersect with and exacerbate existing digital inequalities. What makes this article particularly compelling is its move beyond the traditional “digital divide” framework focusing largely on access toward a more sophisticated understanding of usage, literacy and power asymmetries. This shift is crucial in an era where access to digital tools is increasingly widespread, but the ability to meaningfully engage with and benefit from these technologies remains unevenly distributed (Lutz, 2019). One of the articles strengths is shown in how Lutz highlights how algorithmic systems and data driven technologies can reproduce and even deepen social biases, making these systems and technologies not neutral. Lutz effectively draws attention to the ways in which AI applications, often perceived as neutral or objective, may embed discrimination, specifically against certain groups due to biased training data studies that technological advancements do not inherently lead to fairer or more democratic societies. The article contributes to a growing body of literature concerned with the ethical and social implications of algorithmic governance (Eubank, 2018).

A key strength of the article lies in its global perspective. Kasapis uses examples ranging from UN declarations to national court rulings such as the 2016 ruling by the UN Human Rights Counsil that condemned intentional internet disruptions, which reinforces the argument that digital access is increasingly being recognised as essential to exercising freedoms of expression, assembly and information. This interlinking of rights underpins the authors thesis that internet access is a gateway right, one that enables the realization of many others. Another element is the articles attention to contemporary relevance. Since it was published in the year 2020, it echoes the heightened urgency brought by the COVID-19 pandemic, during which online access became a lifeline for education, work, healthcare and even civic engagement (Kasapis, 2020). Kasapis uses this context not only to illustrate the practical importance of connectivity but to also challenge outdated conceptions of infrastructure. The author argues that the internet should be treated like electricity or water, which is a public utility essential for modern citizenship (Kasapis, 2020).

While this article presents a strong conceptual framework it could benefit from more empirical grounding. Many of the claims about AI-related inequalities are valid but remain abstract. Case studies or real-world examples such as algorithmic bias in credit scoring, predictive policing or employment would have made the argument more tangible and impactful. Similarly, while the call for improved digital literacy and public policy interventions is persuasive, there is little exploration of what successful interventions might look like or how they could be implemented in practice. Another part that could be expanded is the global perspective. Much of the discussion seems implicitly centered Western or developed countries, where digital infrastructure is well established. It does not substantially engage with how digital inequalities manifest in the Global South, where disparities in access and usage are often far more severe and connected with issues of surveillance, colonialism, and resource scarcity (Couldry & Mejias, 2019). A more global lens would have enriched the discussion and acknowledged the uneven geographies of AI development and deployment.

Overall, while Lutz rightly calls for greater digital literacy and public policy engagement , the article remains somewhat vague on specific solutions. Lutz article makes a valuable contribution by reframing digital inequalities within the context of AI and big data. It serves as a call to action for scholars, policymakers and technologists to rethink how we build and govern these technologies, not just for efficiency or profit but with equity and justice at the core. Future research could build on this work by exploring practical, interdisciplinary interventions aimed at promoting algorithmic accountability and equitable digital participation. As AI systems become more embedded in everyday life, these concerns will only grow in importance.
